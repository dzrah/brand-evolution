An evolution, not a rebrand.

The Space42 brand book is solid as a foundation. It defines the merger story, the monolithic logo, the all-blue palette, the typography pair, and a small custom icon set. It is mono-blue, monoglot, and silent.

This proposal adds the layers that are missing — without modifying anything that already exists. Every new element is constructed from the same 1×1 unit-of-measure as the logo. No new strategy, no new tagline, no new colour identity. Just the seven chapters needed to make Space42 work across every surface, sound, and screen it now has to live on.

It is meant to be reviewed end-to-end as a living microsite. Each chapter is self-contained, but they share one design language. Comments and changes welcome before we commit anything to production.

Pairing rules · how the families work together3.2.3
Rule 01

Antarctica = display only.

Hero, headlines, eyebrows, big numbers. Never below 24pt. Never more than 5–7 words at display sizes.

Rule 02

Inter = everything else.

Body, captions, UI labels, navigation, buttons. Open-source, screen-optimised, ships with Arabic-aware metrics.

Rule 03

Verdana = Office fallback only.

Internal Word, PowerPoint and email signatures. Retained for backward compatibility — not for design-grade output.

Section 3.3Visual language
Introducing

Graphic
elements.

A proprietary kit of four devices, all extracted from the logo's own construction grid. They give Space42 a visual language that nobody else owns — built on the same 1×1 bar that builds the icon.

The four-element kit3.3.1
01 / 04 · StructureThe Grid

Modular squares and rectangles for structure, backgrounds, and content frames. All sized on multiples of the 1×1 unit.

02 / 04 · MotionSignal Lines

Thin horizontal lines that suggest transmission, frequency, signal-to-noise. Used as quiet rhythm or interrupted by a brand accent.

03 / 04 · AmbitionOrbit Curves

Large sweeping arcs that suggest planetary scale, reach, and ambition. Always partial — only fragments of an orbit ever shown.

04 / 04 · FlagBar Marker

Direct extraction of the icon's two-bar shape, used as a typographic flag. The shortest path to "Space42 was here".

Construction · every element on the same 1×1 unit3.3.3
Rules · binding

One unit of measure. Six rules.

  • Every element scales in multiples of the logo's 1×1 bar.
  • Max two elements per composition. Never three.
  • Blue42 + White own 80% of every surface. Contrast colours appear only as accents.
  • Lines are always 1px or a multiple of the unit. Never blurry hairlines.
  • Orbits are always partial. Never closed loops.
  • The bar marker keeps the same proportions as the logo. Never rotated, never vertical, never recoloured outside palette.

Audio.

Space42 is producing increasing volumes of video — corporate films, product reveals, social cuts, partner content. Today most of it leaves the studio without a sound that's recognisably Space42. This chapter proposes a sonic identity to close that gap. Nothing here is produced yet — every track, sound and voiceover is a direction to commission with an external sonic-branding partner.

The brand has a face. It is still waiting for a voice.

Every Space42 video, every product reveal, every keynote currently borrows generic library music and unattributed voiceover. Audio is the one brand surface that is still anonymous. As we publish more film, the gap gets louder.

Section 3.5Motion
Introducing

Animated logo
+ icons.

The logo learns to move — by revealing its own construction. Bars assemble. The wordmark wipes in. Sound and image sync. No bounce, no spring, no 3D — just geometric precision, like the static identity in time.

Master logo animation · 2.0–2.5s canonical reveal3.5.1
00:02 / 00:02

The bars build the icon. The wordmark wipes in. Then everything settles.

Each bar is atomic — it scales and translates, but never morphs into another shape. Motion is always in one direction per beat. Easing is geometric (cubic-bezier .22, 1, .36, 1) — no overshoot, no bounce.

The sonic logo is frame-synchronised: the chord lands when the wordmark resolves.

before we commit.

Each of these needs stakeholder sign-off before the relevant chapter goes to design development. None of them block the others — they can be resolved in parallel with Phase 1.

Audio · Tier 4 Q·01

Arabic voiceover: ElevenLabs Multilingual, or human-only?

Multilingual is cheaper, faster and consistent with Leo's character. Human-only is warmer, regionally authentic, and never breaks pronunciation.

ElevenLabs MLv2 Human-only Hybrid
Audio · Tier 1 Q·02

Sonic logo: provide reference tracks to the studio, or descriptive only?

References give the studio a head-start but can anchor the output too tightly. Descriptive only is freer but riskier and slower.

References Descriptive only
Motion · Section 3.5 Q·03

Icon idle: persistent breathing pulse, or static?

Pulse adds presence and "alive" signal. Static is more stable, less distracting, easier to A/B against perceived performance.

Persistent pulse Static Scroll-triggered
Paperwork · Section 3.6 Q·04

Cover aesthetic: product photography / CGI, or abstract graphic elements?

Product-forward builds confidence in the hardware. Brand-forward builds the system. Probably a tier-by-tier mix — fact sheets product-forward, catalogues brand-forward.

Product-forward Brand-forward Tier-mixed
UI library · Section 3.7 Q·05

Design-system name: sub-brand ("Beyond"), or generic ("Space42 DS")?

A sub-brand name gives the system identity and rallies teams. A generic name keeps Space42 monolithic and avoids fragmentation.
